# Expert Takeaways Reference

Synthesize expert interviews into a tight narrative memo. Each bullet is a standalone insight with claim, mechanism, and implication.

## When to Use

- After completing expert interviews for a market
- When synthesizing 2-5 expert perspectives into unified takeaways
- For executive-ready summaries that feel interview-grounded

## Core Principles

### 1. Single-Idea Bullets

Each bullet carries **one** main insight with one mechanism. If you're tempted to use "also" or "meanwhile" – split into separate bullets.

**Good**: One claim, one causal chain, one implication
**Bad**: Multiple observations packed into a paragraph

### 2. Claim → Mechanism → Implication

Every bullet follows this structure:
- **Claim**: What's happening (lead with this)
- **Mechanism**: Why it happens / how it works
- **Implication**: So what for the client

```
Collecting's emotional core is being hollowed out by instant access. [CLAIM]
Both experts describe how "digging" – visiting physical stores, talking to
shop owners, researching through limited channels – used to create attachment.
AI and instant search have eliminated that process. [MECHANISM]
Without invested time, collecting becomes indistinguishable from consumption. [IMPLICATION]
```

### 3. Lead with the Claim

First sentence is the takeaway. Don't set scene first; don't build to the insight.

**Good**: "Sneakers, toys, and cards aren't separate markets – they're one tribe moving between categories."

**Bad**: "In our interviews, experts discussed how collector behavior manifests across categories. What emerged was a pattern suggesting that..."

### 4. Diagnostic Voice, Not Prescriptive

This is "what experts observed" – not "what the client should do." Keep mode consistent throughout.

**Good**: "The strategic opportunity is concentration, not diversification."
**Bad**: "Jordan should concentrate, not diversify." (too directive)

**Good**: "Both experts warn against chasing categories established players own."
**Bad**: "Don't chase categories." (imperative without attribution)

### 5. Attribution Keeps It Interview-Authentic

Use phrases that ground insights in the research:
- "Both experts describe..."
- "Experts observe a pattern where..."
- "According to [Expert], ..."
- "In practice, this manifests as..."

Avoid asserting universal truths as fact. Soften where appropriate:
- "tends to hold" vs "holds"
- "Experts describe a hierarchy" vs "Value follows a clear hierarchy"

### 6. Concrete Anchors Without Bloat

Include specific proof points that make claims believable:
- Demographics: "male, 25-45, with disposable income"
- Numbers: "1.08M RMB," "Olympic four-year rhythms"
- Examples: "Labubu," "The Last Dance," "Luka, Shai, Morant"

But don't overload. One or two anchors per bullet is enough.

### 7. Economy + Punch

Short, high-contrast constructions work best:
- "looks like X, but isn't"
- "not A – B"
- "When X, Y follows"

Trim connective tissue. Every word should earn its place.

## Quality Checklist

For each bullet:
- [ ] Opens with the claim (not scene-setting)
- [ ] Contains only one main insight
- [ ] Has clear mechanism (the "because" or "how")
- [ ] Ends with implication (so what)
- [ ] Attributed or appropriately hedged
- [ ] At least one concrete anchor
- [ ] Consistent mode (diagnostic throughout)

For the full set:
- [ ] 5-8 bullets total (enough to be substantive, few enough to be memorable)
- [ ] No mode-switching (don't mix diagnosis with recommendations)
- [ ] Similar bullet lengths (uniform shape)
- [ ] Reads like interview distillation, not authored essay

## Anti-Patterns

| Avoid | Do Instead |
|-------|------------|
| Multi-idea paragraphs | Split into single-idea bullets |
| Headline + fragment (slide style) | Full sentences with causal logic |
| Slogans without support | Every claim has mechanism |
| Theory voice ("Value follows a clear hierarchy...") | Interview voice ("Experts describe...") |
| Prescriptive imperatives ("Don't chase...") | Diagnostic framing ("Both experts warn against...") |
| Abstract language ("hollowed-out emotional foundation") | Concrete description ("AI and instant search eliminated the process") |
| Building to the insight | Lead with the claim |

## Comparison: Memo vs Slide Style

| Aspect | Interview Takeaway (Memo) | Strategy Slide |
|--------|---------------------------|----------------|
| Structure | Paragraph bullets, full sentences | Headlines + fragments |
| Voice | Analytical, diagnostic | Directive, prescriptive |
| Tone | Observational, nuanced | Assertive, recommendation-forward |
| Sentence form | Complete sentences with rhythm | Compressed clauses, slogan-like |
| Causality | Explicit: "X because Y, leading to Z" | Often implied |
| Reader experience | Believable, walks through reasoning | Easy to scan, needs narration |

**For expert takeaways, use Memo style.** Strategy recommendations come later in the deliverable flow.

## Output Structure

```markdown
---
Type: Expert Synthesis
Market: [Market Name]
Experts:
  - [Expert 1 Name] – [Role/Affiliation]
  - [Expert 2 Name] – [Role/Affiliation]
Date: [Date]
Project: "[[Databases/Projects/project-name/00-status|Project Name]]"
---

# [Market] Expert Interviews – Takeaways

[Bullet 1: Claim → mechanism → implication]

[Bullet 2: Claim → mechanism → implication]

[Bullet 3: Claim → mechanism → implication]

...

[Bullet N: Final insight, often the strategic opportunity or key tension]
```

## Example (Korea)

```markdown
Korea looks like a collector market, but isn't one. What appears as collecting
behavior (queues, sell-outs, bulk buying) is mostly hype-driven consumption.
When resale value collapses, interest evaporates. True collectors exist but
remain an extreme minority.

True collecting begins with "digging" – but that's being eroded. Collectors
accumulate knowledge before objects: learning histories, timelines, variations.
Instant search and social media summaries have removed the pleasure of discovery,
weakening emotional investment and making collecting more shallow and disposable.
```

Note how each bullet:
- Opens with the claim
- Provides mechanism (why/how)
- Lands an implication
- Uses concrete language
- Maintains diagnostic voice throughout
