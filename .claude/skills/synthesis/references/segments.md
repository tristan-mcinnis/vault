# Segmentation Analysis Reference

Identify and characterize distinct consumer segments from qualitative research data.

## Segmentation Dimensions

| Dimension | Examples |
|-----------|----------|
| **Behavioral** | Usage frequency, purchase triggers, channel preference |
| **Attitudinal** | Values, priorities, risk tolerance |
| **Needs-based** | Problems being solved, jobs to be done |
| **Occasion-based** | When/where/why they engage |
| **Lifecycle** | Relationship stage with category |

## Identifying Segment Candidates

Look for:
- Divergent patterns (not everyone behaves the same)
- Clustering of characteristics
- Distinct motivations or barriers
- Different language or framing

Group participants/narratives by:
1. Similar behaviors
2. Similar attitudes
3. Similar needs
4. Similar language/framing

**Aim for 3-5 segments** (too few = not useful; too many = not actionable)

## Segment Profile Components

- **Name**: Memorable, descriptive label
- **Size estimate**: Rough proportion (if quantifiable)
- **Defining characteristic**: The one thing that makes them distinct
- **Behaviors**: What they do
- **Attitudes**: What they believe/value
- **Needs**: What problems they're solving
- **Barriers**: What holds them back
- **Triggers**: What activates them
- **Representative quotes**: Verbatims that capture the segment

## Validation Criteria

Test the segments:
- **Mutually exclusive?** Can place people in one segment
- **Collectively exhaustive?** Cover the population
- **Actionable?** Can you do different things for each?
- **Identifiable?** Can you find them in the real world?

## Quality Guidelines

**Good Segmentation**:
- Segments feel like real, recognizable people
- Clear behavioral/attitudinal differences
- Actionable - can develop different strategies
- Memorable names that stick

**Poor Segmentation**:
- Based only on demographics
- Overlapping segments
- Too abstract to identify in real world
- Names that are jargon-heavy
