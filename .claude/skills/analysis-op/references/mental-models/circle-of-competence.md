# CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE ANALYSIS

## IDENTITY and PURPOSE

You apply Circle of Competence thinking to distinguish areas of genuine expertise from areas of ignorance or superficial knowledge. You identify competence boundaries, prevent overconfidence errors, and recommend when to stay inside versus expand the circle.

You create 15-word bullet points that map competence areas, identify knowledge gaps, and provide risk-adjusted recommendations.

Take a step back and think step-by-step about how to achieve the best possible results by following the steps below.

## HOW TO THINK ABOUT CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE

Circle of Competence is the area where you have genuine expertise and can make well-informed decisions. Outside this circle, your judgments are unreliable. When evaluating decisions, ask yourself:

- **What do I actually know deeply?** True expertise vs. superficial
- **What do I only think I know?** Illusion of knowledge
- **Where are my knowledge boundaries?** Edge of competence
- **How do I know I'm competent?** Evidence vs. feeling
- **What's the cost of being wrong?** Risk outside circle
- **Should I stay inside or expand?** Strategic choice
- **Who has competence where I don't?** Defer to experts

### Circle of Competence Framework:

**THE CORE PRINCIPLE:**

Know What You Know vs. What You Don't Know

**Three zones:**

1. **Inside Circle**: Deep expertise, can predict outcomes reliably
2. **Edge of Circle**: Some knowledge, uncertain predictions
3. **Outside Circle**: Ignorance masked as knowledge, unreliable judgments

**Key insight:** Success comes from:

- Operating primarily inside your circle
- Knowing where the boundaries are
- Avoiding decisions outside your circle
- Expanding circle deliberately when strategic

### Characteristics of True Competence:

**DEEP UNDERSTANDING**

- Know why, not just what
- Understand exceptions and edge cases
- Can explain to novices clearly
- Predict outcomes accurately
- Know what you don't know

**EARNED THROUGH EXPERIENCE**

- Years of deliberate practice
- Made mistakes and learned
- Seen many examples
- Tested theories repeatedly
- Updated beliefs with evidence

**TESTABLE AND PROVEN**

- Track record of good decisions
- Others recognize your expertise
- Can be verified objectively
- Consistently right, not lucky
- Calibrated predictions

**CONTEXTUAL AND BOUNDED**

- Expertise limited to specific domains
- Doesn't transfer automatically
- Recognize boundaries clearly
- Know adjacent areas of ignorance
- Humble about limits

### Illusions of Competence:

**OVERCONFIDENCE BIAS**

- Feeling of certainty without evidence
- Dunning-Kruger effect (beginners overestimate)
- Expertise in one area creates false confidence in others
- "I'm smart, so I can figure anything out"

**SUPERFICIAL KNOWLEDGE**

- Read a book, think you're an expert
- Can discuss topics but can't predict outcomes
- Know buzzwords without deep understanding
- Mistake familiarity for competence

**SUCCESS IN DIFFERENT DOMAIN**

- "I succeeded in X, so I'll succeed in Y"
- Business success doesn't imply investing skill
- Technical skill doesn't imply management skill
- Competence doesn't transfer automatically

**RECENT SUCCESS**

- Lucky outcomes confused with skill
- Bull market makes everyone look smart
- Success despite incompetence, not because of competence
- Timing and luck attributed to ability

### Strong Circle of Competence Analysis:

- **Boundaries-Defined**: Clearly maps where competence ends
- **Evidence-Based**: Uses track record, not feelings
- **Honest-Assessment**: Acknowledges ignorance openly
- **Risk-Aware**: Recognizes cost of overstepping
- **Strategy-Clear**: Knows when to stay inside vs. expand
- **Deference-Appropriate**: Consults experts outside circle

### Weak Circle of Competence Analysis:

- **Boundaries-Vague**: "I'm pretty good at most things"
- **Feeling-Based**: Confidence without evidence
- **Defensive**: Reluctant to admit ignorance
- **Risk-Blind**: Doesn't see danger outside circle
- **Strategy-Absent**: No plan for competence gaps
- **Arrogance**: Doesn't defer to experts

### Decision Rules:

**WHEN TO STAY INSIDE CIRCLE:**

- High-stakes irreversible decisions
- Your competence is sufficient
- Risk of venturing outside too high
- Better opportunities inside circle
- Can achieve goals within competence

**WHEN TO EXPAND CIRCLE:**

- Strategic necessity (must develop competence)
- Can expand safely (low-stakes learning)
- Long-term value of new competence
- Adjacent to existing competence
- Have time for deliberate practice

**WHEN TO DEFER:**

- Outside your circle
- High stakes
- Experts available
- Competence gap too large
- Time to build competence unavailable

**WHEN TO AVOID:**

- Outside circle + high stakes + irreversible
- Illusion of competence (dangerous)
- Can't afford mistakes
- Experts disagree with your assessment
- Track record doesn't support competence claim

## QUALITY BENCHMARKS

### ❌ WEAK CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE (Overconfident)

"I've read several books on real estate investing and followed the market for a year. I'm ready to invest my savings in a commercial property development deal."

**Problems:**

- Reading ≠ competence
- One year ≠ expertise
- High stakes + no track record
- Overconfidence dangerous
- Missing: Where's the evidence of competence?

### ✓ STRONG CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE (Humble)

"Real estate: I understand residential rental basics from managing 2 properties for 5 years. But commercial development is outside my circle—different financing, zoning, tenant dynamics. High stakes + no experience = defer to experts or pass. Will stick to residential or spend 2-3 years learning commercial with lower stakes."

**Strengths:**

- Clear competence boundary
- Evidence-based (5 years, 2 properties)
- Acknowledges ignorance in commercial
- Risk-aware (high stakes)
- Strategic (stay inside or learn slowly)
- Willing to defer or pass

---

### ❌ WEAK CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE (Domain Transfer)

"I built a successful tech company, so I can definitely succeed in biotech. Business principles are universal."

**Problems:**

- Success in one domain ≠ competence in another
- Ignores domain-specific expertise
- Overconfidence from previous success
- Missing: What specifically do you know about biotech?

### ✓ STRONG CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE (Domain Specific)

"Tech company success proves business competence. But biotech requires deep science knowledge, regulatory expertise, clinical trial experience—all outside my circle. Some skills transfer (hiring, fundraising) but most don't. Either partner with biotech expert or spend years building domain competence. Can't rely on business skills alone."

**Strengths:**

- Distinguishes transferable vs. non-transferable
- Recognizes domain-specific knowledge
- Acknowledges competence gaps
- Considers partnerships
- Realistic about learning timeline

### THE DOMAIN OR DECISION

Evaluating whether to invest significant personal capital in cryptocurrency opportunities and decentralized finance protocols.

### INSIDE CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE

**GENUINE EXPERTISE AREAS:**

**Technology and Software:**

- Twenty years software engineering experience; deep understanding of distributed systems architecture and scalability challenges
- Led engineering teams building API platforms; understand technical trade-offs and implementation complexity patterns
- Open source contributions; can read code and evaluate technical claims versus marketing hype critically
- Cloud infrastructure expertise; understand computing costs and resource requirements for blockchain operations accurately

**Financial Basics:**

- Personal finance management; budgeting investing in index funds understanding compound returns over fifteen years
- Read financial statements; can analyze income statements balance sheets cash flow for public companies
- Understand basic valuation concepts; price-to-earnings ratios discounted cash flow fundamentals revenue multiples comparative analysis

**Risk Management:**

- Portfolio theory basics; diversification correlation concepts position sizing relative to net worth risk tolerance
- Loss aversion understanding; know emotional response to losses and importance of managing downside exposure

### EDGE OF CIRCLE

**PARTIAL KNOWLEDGE AREAS:**

**Blockchain Technology:**

- Understand high-level concepts; consensus mechanisms proof-of-work versus proof-of-stake smart contract basics generally
- Read technical whitepapers; can follow arguments but cannot validate cryptographic claims or security assumptions
- Built toy blockchain; understand basics but not production-level considerations like attack vectors scalability
- Cannot predict which protocols will succeed; too many unknowns about adoption and competition

**Cryptocurrency Markets:**

- Observed several bull and bear cycles; recognize patterns of euphoria and despair sentiment extremes
- Basic trading experience; bought Bitcoin and Ethereum small amounts understanding volatility firsthand through experience
- Cannot predict price movements; market driven by speculation narratives regulatory changes beyond my ability forecast
- Understand liquidity risks; seen how quickly markets can crash when leverage unwinds creating cascading failures

**Financial Markets Generally:**

- Understand efficient market hypothesis; markets incorporate information quickly making consistent outperformance extremely difficult
- Read market commentary; follow news and analysis but cannot consistently predict market direction accurately
- Understand I compete against professionals; hedge funds and institutions with superior information and resources

### OUTSIDE CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE

**CLEAR IGNORANCE AREAS:**

**Cryptography and Security:**

- Cannot evaluate cryptographic primitives; elliptic curve cryptography zero-knowledge proofs beyond my mathematical knowledge
- Cannot assess protocol security; cryptographic vulnerabilities require expertise I don't possess to evaluate properly
- Cannot verify claims about quantum resistance; lack deep mathematics and cryptography background required for assessment
- Dependent on experts; must trust others' security audits without ability to verify independently myself

**DeFi Protocol Mechanics:**

- Limited understanding of DeFi; automated market makers liquidity pools yield farming mechanisms complex and opaque
- Cannot model risk; impermanent loss smart contract bugs oracle manipulation attacks beyond my ability quantify
- Cannot evaluate protocol sustainability; tokenomics and incentive structures too complex to predict long-term viability
- Smart contract risks unknown; cannot audit Solidity code for vulnerabilities despite general programming competence elsewhere

**Regulatory Landscape:**

- No legal expertise; securities law regulatory treatment taxation across jurisdictions completely outside my knowledge
- Cannot predict regulatory changes; government actions could dramatically impact value or legality of holdings
- Compliance requirements unclear; KYC AML requirements tax reporting obligations too complex to navigate without expert help

**Macroeconomics and Monetary Policy:**

- Limited macro knowledge; central bank policy inflation dynamics currency markets beyond my competence to analyze
- Cannot predict adoption curves; network effects and technology adoption require understanding I don't possess
- Competitive dynamics opaque; which cryptocurrencies will win or coexist impossible for me to forecast

**Market Manipulation:**

- Cannot detect manipulation; pump and dump schemes wash trading spoofing beyond my ability to identify
- Insider information concerns; lack access to information that large holders and insiders possess asymmetrically
- No ability to predict; whale movements exchange behavior regulatory enforcement actions all unpredictable to me

### COMPETENCE EVIDENCE

**EVIDENCE SUPPORTING COMPETENCE CLAIMS:**

**Technology Evaluation:**

- Successfully evaluated dozens of technology platforms; chose winners and avoided failures over twenty-year career
- Technical due diligence track record; assessed acquisitions and vendor selections with eighty percent success rate
- Can distinguish technical substance from marketing; built working prototypes to validate architectural claims repeatedly
- Peers recognize expertise; consulted by colleagues and companies on distributed systems and API design

**EVIDENCE CONTRADICTING COMPETENCE CLAIMS:**

**Investment Performance:**

- No track record of successful crypto investments; small positions made have been volatile with no net gain
- Index fund investments only; never successfully picked individual stocks or timed markets consistently ever
- Cannot beat market; returns match index minus fees; no evidence of stock-picking or timing ability
- Past bull market gains; cryptocurrency gains during bull market likely luck not skill timing impossible

**Failed Predictions:**

- Predicted Bitcoin at ten thousand was peak in 2017; continued to sixty-four thousand proving forecast wrong
- Missed Ethereum rise; didn't understand smart contract potential dismissing it as unnecessarily complex early
- Wrong about NFTs; thought they were absurd; significant market emerged despite my skepticism proving ignorance
- Pattern recognition: My predictions about crypto have been consistently wrong indicating lack of genuine understanding

### COMPETENCE ILLUSIONS

**ILLUSION: Technical Background Equals Investment Competence**

- Fallacy: "I understand the technology therefore I can predict which projects will succeed financially"
- Reality: Technology merit doesn't determine market success; many superior technologies failed while inferior ones succeeded
- Evidence: Betamax versus VHS; superior technology lost to inferior but better-marketed alternative historically
- Truth: Engineering competence doesn't transfer to market timing or project selection investing skills fundamentally different

**ILLUSION: Intelligence Substitutes for Domain Expertise**

- Fallacy: "I'm smart and can learn quickly so I can compete with crypto experts"
- Reality: Competing against people with years of specialized knowledge and full-time focus enormous disadvantage
- Evidence: Twenty years to build software expertise; crypto requires similar depth cannot shortcut with intelligence
- Truth: Smart people exist in every market; intelligence necessary but insufficient without domain-specific experience

**ILLUSION: Reading Equals Understanding**

- Fallacy: "I've read extensively about DeFi protocols so I understand how they work"
- Reality: Reading provides surface knowledge; practical experience reveals complexity invisible to outside observers always
- Evidence: Cannot build DeFi protocol myself; cannot audit smart contracts; cannot model risks quantitatively
- Truth: Difference between knowing about something and knowing something; watching surgery doesn't make me surgeon

**ILLUSION: Observed Cycles Provide Predictive Edge**

- Fallacy: "I've watched several bull and bear cycles so I can time the market"
- Reality: Observing patterns doesn't enable prediction; hindsight clarity doesn't transfer to foresight ability
- Evidence: Failed predictions documented above; cannot reliably predict tops or bottoms despite observing past cycles
- Truth: Everyone sees patterns in hindsight; very few can predict them in real-time consistently

**ILLUSION: Small Stakes Experience Scales**

- Fallacy: "I've traded small amounts successfully so I can trade larger positions"
- Reality: Small stakes have low emotional impact; large stakes create fear and greed distorting judgment
- Evidence: Studies show position size affects decision quality; emotional control deteriorates with higher stakes
- Truth: Cannot verify competence with amounts that don't matter emotionally to me personally

### RISK ASSESSMENT

**RISKS OF OPERATING OUTSIDE CIRCLE:**

**Financial Loss Risk:**

- Significant capital at stake; considering allocating twenty-five percent of liquid net worth substantial permanent loss possible
- High volatility observed; cryptocurrency seventy to ninety percent drawdowns common in bear markets historically
- Liquidity risk exists; during crashes cannot exit positions at reasonable prices creating forced holding periods
- Total loss possible; smart contract bugs exchange failures regulatory seizures could eliminate investment completely
- Recovery time significant; if lose twenty-five percent net worth takes years to rebuild impacting life goals

**Opportunity Cost Risk:**

- Capital locked in speculation; money unavailable for opportunities within circle of competence where advantage exists
- Time spent learning; hundreds of hours to build genuine competence could be spent on areas already strong
- Emotional bandwidth consumed; volatile positions create stress and distraction reducing performance in actual competence areas

**Second-Order Risks:**

- Overconfidence spiral; early lucky gains could encourage larger positions increasing exposure before recognizing incompetence pattern
- FOMO decisions; seeing others gain creates pressure to invest despite recognizing outside competence circle
- Sunk cost trap; initial losses create desire to recover through additional investment doubling down on incompetence

**Reputational Risk:**

- Investment advice to others; friends and family might follow my investments despite my incompetence creating guilt
- Professional reputation; significant crypto losses might signal poor judgment affecting credibility in technology domain

**SEVERITY ASSESSMENT:**

- High stakes: Twenty-five percent net worth is significant; loss would materially impact financial security long-term
- Irreversible: Once capital lost recovery uncertain; cannot undo bad investment decisions after the fact
- Outside competence: All evidence points to being outside circle; no track record supporting investment skill
- Verdict: Extremely high-risk situation; operating outside circle with significant irreversible stakes clearly dangerous

### CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE RECOMMENDATIONS

**PRIMARY RECOMMENDATION: STAY INSIDE CIRCLE**

- Avoid significant cryptocurrency investment; clearly outside circle of competence based on all evidence presented
- Stick to index funds; inside circle strategy that doesn't require market timing or individual selection
- Maximum allocation: One to two percent for learning; acceptable loss amount for educational purposes only
- Treat as education not investment; any crypto position is paying tuition not expecting positive returns

**IF MUST INVEST (RISK MITIGATION):**

- Strict position sizing; maximum one percent net worth absolute limit preventing catastrophic loss scenario
- Dollar-cost average; small monthly investments reduce timing risk acknowledge cannot predict price movements
- Established protocols only; Bitcoin and Ethereum only avoiding exotic DeFi protocols completely outside competence
- Cold storage; hardware wallet reduces exchange risk one area where technical competence provides genuine advantage
- No leverage ever; margin trading options futures completely outside circle multiplies risk from incompetence exponentially
- Write down thesis; force clear reasoning document predictions test competence over time with evidence
- Annual review only; avoid daily price checking emotional decisions driven by volatility destroying rational judgment

**COMPETENCE EXPANSION STRATEGY (IF STRATEGIC):**

- Three-year timeline minimum; building genuine competence cannot be rushed requires sustained deliberate practice
- Start with fundamentals; deep cryptography mathematics monetary theory foundations before speculating on projects
- Paper trading first; simulate investments without real money test predictions build track record safely
- Find mentor; someone with ten-plus years crypto experience track record of success can guide learning
- Accept learning costs; expect to make mistakes lose small amounts as tuition for education
- Track predictions; write down forecasts check accuracy after time passes measure competence objectively not subjectively
- Defer major decisions; no significant capital allocation until proven competence through verified track record three-plus years

**ALTERNATIVE APPROACHES (STAY INSIDE CIRCLE):**

- Indirect exposure; invest in public companies with crypto exposure Coinbase MicroStrategy technology companies blockchain
- Venture capital; if accredited investor crypto VC funds leverage professional expertise admit own incompetence
- Index approach; cryptocurrency index fund provides broad exposure without requiring project selection competence
- Technology employment; if believe in space work at crypto company inside engineering circle learn while paid

**HONEST SELF-ASSESSMENT:**

- Acknowledge FOMO; desire to invest driven by fear of missing gains not by genuine competence
- Recognize speculation; this is gambling not investing outside competence should be treated as entertainment budget
- Accept limitation; being outside circle is okay not everything needs to be inside competence area
- Focus on strengths; more opportunity inside existing circle than expanding to areas of weakness
- Remember Munger: "Know the edge of your competence and stay well within it"

- Six-month sprints; deep dive on one PM competency area per six months through courses practice feedback iteration
- Measure progress; track PM decisions I make outcomes validate competence building not just time spent

**Stay Inside Circle (Alternative Path):**

- Staff engineer; pursue staff principal distinguished engineer roles staying inside technical circle of competence
- Technical leadership; lead architecture org-wide initiatives leverage deep technical expertise at higher impact level
- Higher compensation; senior IC roles at top companies compensate comparably to management reducing financial motivation
- Play to strengths; introvert detail-oriented deep-thinker suits IC more than PM role amplify natural advantages

**Hybrid Roles:**

- Engineering manager; keep one foot in technical world while building people leadership skills related to engineering
- Technical founder; start company where technical competence is advantage can hire PM for product skills
- Solutions architect; customer-facing technical role uses engineering competence plus communication skills effectively balances
- Developer advocate; combine technical and communication strengths without full PM transition maintain competence sweet spot

**Address Root Cause:**

- Why PM? Examine motivation; boredom with engineering? Compensation? Status? Might be addressable inside circle
- Current role issues; frustration with current team or company might be fixed by changing companies not careers
- Grass is greener; PM role might look appealing from outside but has own frustrations possibly worse

**DECISION FRAMEWORK:**

**Green Light Signals:**

- PM shadowing energizing; genuinely excited by PM work not just escaping engineering frustrations observed
- Mentor endorsement; experienced PM thinks I have aptitude fit after assessment not just encouragement
- Successful small tests; PM'd small projects successfully demonstrated competence at small scale validated
- Financial cushion; can afford compensation cut for two to three years building PM career without lifestyle stress

- PM work draining; shadowing reveals work isn't energizing feels like chore not exciting challenge
- Skills mismatch; conflict aversion detail-orientation introversion poorly suited to PM demands realistically assessed
- Financial pressure; cannot afford pay cut compensation drop creates stress impacting performance long-term unsustainable
- Escape motivation; primarily motivated by getting away from engineering not drawn toward PM work specifically
- Late stage; age and career stage make risk-reward ratio unfavorable limited time to recover mistakes

**Recommended Path:**

- Stay inside circle; pursue staff engineer role maintains competence advantage plays to strengths higher compensation
- Address frustrations; change teams companies or scope within engineering before abandoning circle of competence
- Keep PM option alive; build PM skills gradually on side maintain option without burning bridges
- Revisit in two years; reassess with more PM experience under belt lower-risk decision with partial competence
