# Behavioral Economics Insight Extraction: The Kahneman Lens

This framework aims to uncover insights from qualitative research that leverage behavioral economics principles to understand consumer decision-making.

## Core Principles:

1. **System 1 vs. System 2 Thinking**: Identify fast, intuitive vs. slow, deliberate decision processes.
2. **Cognitive Biases**: Look for evidence of common biases affecting consumer choices.
3. **Prospect Theory**: Consider how framing of gains and losses influences decisions.
4. **Anchoring**: Seek insights on how initial information affects subsequent judgments.
5. **Availability Heuristic**: Identify how easily recalled information impacts perceptions.
6. **Choice Architecture**: Look for ways to influence decisions through option presentation.

## HOW TO THINK LIKE A BEHAVIORAL ECONOMIST

Behavioral economics reveals the gap between “rational” decision-making and actual human behavior. When analyzing transcripts:

### Key Questions to Ask:

**System 1 vs. System 2:**
- When do consumers use mental shortcuts vs. deliberate analysis?
- What triggers automatic responses vs. careful consideration?
- Look for: “I just…”, “without thinking”, “instinctively” (System 1) vs. “I researched”, “compared”, “calculated” (System 2)

**Cognitive Biases:**
- **Anchoring**: Does initial information (price, feature, comparison) disproportionately influence judgment?
- **Availability**: Do recent experiences or vivid examples dominate decision-making?
- **Confirmation Bias**: Do consumers seek information that confirms existing beliefs?
- **Status Quo Bias**: Is inertia preventing change even when alternatives are better?
- **Loss Aversion**: Are losses weighted more heavily than equivalent gains?

**Framing Effects:**
- How do consumers respond differently to equivalent options presented differently?
- “90% fat-free” vs. “10% fat” — same facts, different emotional response
- Gain framing (“save $50”) vs. loss framing (“don’t lose $50”)

**Choice Architecture:**
- How does option presentation affect decisions?
- Default options, number of choices, order of presentation matter

### Don’t Just Describe — Name the Mechanism:

❌ **Weak**: “Consumers prefer the first option they see”
✓ **Strong**: “Anchoring to initial price point creates reference frame biasing subsequent value judgments”

❌ **Weak**: “People make quick decisions at checkout”
✓ **Strong**: “Cognitive depletion after shopping activates System 1 impulse purchasing bypass deliberation”

## Analysis Process:

7. Extract 20-50 key observations from the transcript, focusing on:
    - Decision-making processes and shortcuts
    - Evidence of cognitive biases in consumer behavior
    - Framing effects on product or brand perceptions
    - Anchoring influences in pricing or product comparisons
    - Availability of information affecting consumer choices
    - Choice presentation and its impact on decisions
8. Synthesize into 10-25 actionable insights that:
    - Reveal opportunities to leverage System 1 thinking in marketing
    - Suggest ways to mitigate or utilize cognitive biases
    - Identify effective framing strategies for products or messages
    - Highlight potential anchoring tactics in pricing or positioning
    - Inform strategies to make key information more available
    - Offer approaches to optimize choice architecture

## QUALITY BENCHMARKS

### ❌ WEAK INSIGHT (Descriptive, Not Theoretical)

“Consumers tend to buy items displayed at eye level more frequently than other placements”

**Problems:**
- Describes behavior without explaining psychological mechanism
- Could be written without any behavioral economics knowledge
- Missing the “why” that makes it actionable
- No connection to broader principles

### ✓ STRONG INSIGHT (Theory-Applied)

“Eye-level placement reduces cognitive effort triggering System 1 selection; vertical search activates deliberation”

**Strengths:**
- Names the mechanism (System 1 vs. cognitive effort)
- Explains why placement matters psychologically
- Implies strategic action (make preferred options easy to find)
- Connects observation to theoretical framework

---

### ❌ WEAK INSIGHT (Obvious Without Theory)

“Consumers sometimes make different choices depending on how information is presented to them”

**Problems:**
- “Sometimes” is vague
- No specific framing example
- States the obvious
- Not actionable

### ✓ STRONG INSIGHT (Framing-Specific)

“Loss-framed messaging drives insurance purchases; gain-framed messaging fails as protection prevents loss”

**Strengths:**
- Identifies specific bias (loss aversion)
- Category-specific application (insurance)
- Explains why one frame works and another doesn’t
- Directly actionable for messaging

## EXAMPLES

## Example 1: Coffee Purchase Behavior

### TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT

“I usually just grab whatever coffee is on sale at the grocery store. I know there are better brands out there, but honestly, I can’t tell the difference most mornings when I’m rushing to work. Sometimes I’ll splurge on the fancy stuff if it’s a weekend and I have time to actually enjoy it.”

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS ANALYSIS

**System 1 vs. System 2:**
- Weekday: System 1 dominates under time pressure → price heuristic
- Weekend: System 2 engages when cognitive resources available → quality evaluation

**Cognitive State:**
- Morning rush = cognitive depletion → simplified decision rules
- Leisure time = cognitive availability → willingness to deliberate

**Anchoring:**
- “On sale” creates price anchor determining value perception
- Weekend context resets quality anchor

### SAMPLE OBSERVATIONS

- Time pressure activates price-based heuristics bypassing quality evaluation in coffee selection
- Cognitive load from morning routine depletes resources needed for taste differentiation assessment
- Weekend leisure state enables System 2 processing making quality premiums justifiable to consumers
- Context switching between weekday and weekend creates dual decision frameworks for identical product

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS INSIGHTS

- Cognitive depletion triggers default to price heuristics; available mental bandwidth determines premiumization willingness
- System 1 price anchoring dominates rushed contexts; leisure time activates quality deliberation
- Decision fatigue from morning routines activates simplification heuristics eliminating quality consideration pathways

## Example 2: Fitness App Abandonment

### TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT

“I’ve downloaded like five different fitness apps, and I use them for maybe a week and then forget about them. It’s not that they’re bad—they just feel like homework. I already have enough pressure in my life without my phone nagging me to exercise.”

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS ANALYSIS

**Loss Aversion:**
- Notifications framed as obligations create psychological “loss” (freedom, autonomy)
- Apps trigger loss aversion by highlighting gaps (didn’t meet goal = loss)

**Present Bias:**
- Immediate cost (effort, time, discomfort) vs. delayed benefit (fitness)
- Present bias makes current effort loom larger than future health

**Framing Problem:**
- Apps frame exercise as achievement deficit (what you haven’t done)
- Should reframe as gain (what you’ll gain from doing it)

**Choice Architecture:**
- Notifications as defaults create pressure, not motivation
- Opt-in vs. opt-out structure matters

### SAMPLE OBSERVATIONS

- Notification-based engagement triggers loss aversion as gaps become salient generating obligation pressure
- Achievement tracking activates present bias; immediate effort costs exceed delayed fitness benefits
- Gamification mechanics frame non-completion as losses triggering avoidance rather than approach motivation
- Default notification settings create reactance; perceived autonomy loss triggers app abandonment behavior

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS INSIGHTS

- Loss-framed progress tracking triggers avoidance; apps highlighting deficits activate psychological reactance mechanisms
- Present bias amplified by effort salience; immediate workout costs dwarf distant benefits
- Notification defaults create autonomy threats; opt-in architecture preserves agency reducing abandonment rates

## Example 3: Subscription Service Retention

### TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT

“I’m definitely not using my streaming service enough to justify the cost, but canceling feels like such a hassle. I’d have to find where to cancel, then I’d lose access to that one show I might want to watch someday. It’s easier to just keep paying.”

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS ANALYSIS

**Status Quo Bias:**
- Inertia keeps subscription active despite poor value
- Doing nothing is psychologically easier than taking action

**Loss Aversion:**
- Canceling = losing access (salient loss)
- Saving money = abstract gain (less salient)
- Losses loom larger than equivalent gains

**Cognitive Effort:**
- Cancellation process friction creates barrier
- System 2 effort required discourages action

**Availability Heuristic:**
- “That one show” is mentally available
- Months of non-use are less salient

**Sunk Cost Fallacy:**
- Already paid → feel should use it to justify past spending

### SAMPLE OBSERVATIONS

- Status quo bias maintains subscriptions despite poor value; action costs exceed psychological switching benefits
- Loss aversion makes access removal salient while subscription savings remain abstract and distant
- Cancellation friction deliberately exploits cognitive effort aversion preventing rational cost-benefit analysis completion
- Hypothetical future use cases dominate availability bias despite contradicting actual usage patterns
- Sunk cost thinking perpetuates retention; prior payments create justification pressure for continued spending

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS INSIGHTS

- Status quo bias exploited through friction; cancellation effort exceeds perceived switching gains
- Loss-framed access removal dominates gain-framed savings; asymmetric weighting maintains poor-value subscriptions
- Availability heuristic favors rare hypothetical use over frequent non-use; vivid possibilities bias retention

## Example 4: Insurance Shopping

### TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT

“Every insurance site asks for the same information over and over. By the third site I’m exhausted and just stick with what I have, even though I know I’m probably overpaying. It’s not worth the mental energy.”

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS ANALYSIS

**Decision Fatigue:**
- Repeated data entry depletes cognitive resources
- Each additional form increases System 2 load
- Eventually triggers abandonment and default to status quo

**Effort Discounting:**
- Present effort (form completion) weighted heavily
- Future benefit (savings) discounted and abstract

**Anchoring to Status Quo:**
- Current premium becomes reference point
- Savings framed as gains, but switching effort framed as loss

**Choice Overload:**
- Too many options to compare creates paralysis
- Simplification: stick with current

### SAMPLE OBSERVATIONS

- Repeated data entry creates decision fatigue depleting resources needed for price comparison completion
- Switching effort looms disproportionately large; hyperbolic discounting makes future savings psychologically distant
- Status quo premium anchors value perception; alternatives judged relative to current creating endowment effect
- Comparison complexity triggers choice overload; simplification heuristic defaults to existing provider retention

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS INSIGHTS

- Decision fatigue weaponized through redundant entry; cognitive depletion triggers status quo default
- Present effort costs hyperbolic-discounted against distant savings; temporal bias favors inaction
- Endowment effect on current policy; switching alternatives evaluated asymmetrically against owned reference point

## COMPARISON: Same Data, Different Lenses

### TRANSCRIPT

“I feel guilty buying fast food, but sometimes I’m just too exhausted to cook after work.”

**Kahneman (Behavioral Economics) Lens:**
- Decision fatigue depletes cognitive resources eliminating System 2 nutrition deliberation; defaults to convenience

**Pain Points Lens:**
- Work exhaustion creates conflict between health values and convenience needs in meal choices

**Emotional Triggers Lens:**
- Guilt emerges when convenience-driven behavior conflicts with self-identity as health-conscious consumer

**Godin (Marketing) Lens:**
- Permission marketing opportunity: brands offering guilt-free convenience can build authentic tribe membership

## OUTPUT FORMAT:

- Present only the “INSIGHTS” section
- Use clear, concise bullet points
- Limit each insight to 15 words
- Ensure insights cover diverse aspects of behavioral economics
- Focus on actionable findings that can inform marketing and product strategies
- Always name the specific bias, heuristic, or cognitive mechanism at play

This approach aims to transform qualitative data into insights that leverage behavioral economics principles to better understand and influence consumer decision-making.
