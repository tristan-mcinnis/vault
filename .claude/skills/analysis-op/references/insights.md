# Insights Extraction Reference

Extract meaningful insights that reveal underlying truths from qualitative research data.

## What Makes an Insight?

Insights are not observations. They reveal underlying truths that aren't immediately obvious.

| Observation | Insight |
|-------------|---------|
| "Consumers value quality" | "Premium pricing signals efficacy; consumers distrust discounted health products" |
| "Parents are busy" | "Meal shortcuts trigger parental guilt; convenience brands succeed by reframing speed as caregiving" |
| "People browse online" | "E-commerce carts serve as possibility spaces; conversion metrics miss their emotional utility" |

## Insight Quality Framework

**Strong Insights Are:**
- **Abstracted**: Elevated from specific details to broader human truths
- **Actionable**: Point toward clear opportunities or strategic directions
- **Surprising**: Reveal something non-obvious or challenge conventional thinking
- **Emotionally Resonant**: Connect to deeper human needs, fears, or desires
- **Specific Yet Universal**: Grounded in concrete evidence but applicable beyond one person

**Weak Insights Are:**
- **Obvious**: Simply restate what everyone already knows
- **Vague**: Too general to be meaningful or actionable
- **Descriptive**: Just summarize behavior without explaining why it matters
- **Narrow**: So specific they only apply to one person or situation

## Questions to Surface Insights

- **What's surprising or counterintuitive?** Patterns that challenge assumptions
- **What's the deeper "why"?** Move beyond surface behaviors to motivations
- **What's the tension or contradiction?** Conflicting desires or beliefs
- **What's universally relatable yet uniquely expressed?** Human truth beneath specifics
- **What changes the way we think?** Shifts perspectives or reveals opportunities

## Insight Extraction Process

### Step 1: Ideas Inventory
Extract 20-50 observations, quotes, behaviors from the data

### Step 2: Pattern Recognition
Group similar ideas, look for recurring themes

### Step 3: Abstraction
Elevate from specific instances to broader patterns

### Step 4: So What Test
Ask: Why does this matter? What opportunity does it reveal?

### Step 5: Refinement
Sharpen language, ensure 15-word bullets capture the essence

## Quality Benchmarks

**Weak Insight (avoid)**:
- "Consumers want products that offer good value for their money"
  - States the obvious, no depth, not actionable

**Strong Insight (aim for)**:
- "Premium pricing signals product efficacy; consumers distrust discounted health products as potentially ineffective"
  - Reveals counterintuitive relationship, explains mechanism, actionable

**Weak Insight (avoid)**:
- "Busy parents often struggle to find time for meal preparation"
  - Just describes known behavior, no psychological depth

**Strong Insight (aim for)**:
- "Meal shortcuts trigger parental guilt; convenience brands succeed by reframing speed as caregiving"
  - Identifies emotional tension, reveals strategic opportunity

## Insight Types

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Behavioral** | Why people act as they do | "Cart abandonment serves emotional function of possibility browsing" |
| **Motivational** | What drives decisions | "Time scarcity commodifies premium; consumption context required for premiumization" |
| **Tension** | Conflicting desires | "Wellness apps fail when borrowing productivity mechanics; self-care requires permission not pressure" |
| **Identity** | Self-concept drivers | "Premium gym cost creates commitment motivation; expensive membership drives show-up behavior" |
| **Cultural** | Social/cultural patterns | "Sustainability fatigue drives demand for pre-vetted brands; consumers want defaults not decisions" |
