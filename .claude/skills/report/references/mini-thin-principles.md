# Mini-Thin Principles

The Oreo Mini-Thin oral debrief (Dec 2025) established the methodology for decision documents. This reference captures why that document worked.

## Why It Works

### 1. Headline Architecture

Every headline is a finding, not a topic:

| Slide | Headline | Why It Works |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Strategic Question | Can a sweet biscuit win in munching — a space dominated by savory? | Opens with core problem + answer |
| Mechanism | Why Savory Dominates — And How Sweet Can Win | Competitive framing + how to beat it |
| Soul Question | Why Not Just Put This Flavor on Mini Oreo? | Anticipates pushback head-on |
| Formula | Thin crispy biscuit + Sweet-sour filling = Removes sweetness ceiling | Crystallizes insight into memorable equation |
| Occasion | Mini-Thin Shines in We-Time More Than Me-Time | Specific, counter-intuitive finding |
| Format | Packaging Format Dictates Social Permission | Insight about the choice, not just options |
| Recommendations | Start with Social Gatherings | Actionable, ranked |
| Bottom Line | 清爽不腻 | One memorable takeaway in consumer language |

### 2. Soul Questions

The Mini-Thin document explicitly addressed the skeptic's challenge:

> "If refreshing sweet-sour flavor removes the munching ceiling, why not just add it to Mini Oreo?"

This prevents weeks of post-meeting debate. The answer:

> "Architecture makes the flavor land. Mini-Thin is a new product, not a line extension."

**Principle:** Every major recommendation should anticipate and answer the obvious objection.

### 3. Tables Over Prose

The document used tables for:
- Comparisons (Savory vs. Sweet vs. Mini-Thin)
- Evaluations (Munchability scorecard)
- Decisions ("If Box → What it signals → What you need")
- Portfolio positioning (Mini Oreo vs. Mini-Thin)

**Principle:** If it's a comparison or evaluation, put it in a table. Prose obscures structure.

> **PPTX caveat:** Tables on slides must be sparse: maximum 3 columns, 4 rows. If the table exceeds this, simplify to essential dimensions or move the full table to speaker notes and show only the verdict on the slide. The Mini-Thin oral debrief tables worked because the presenter narrated the cells live. In a standalone slide deck, dense tables become unreadable.

### 4. Consumer Language as Evidence

Chinese verbatims appeared throughout:
- 解馋 (temper the craving)
- 嘴巴寂寞 (my mouth feels bored)
- 清爽不腻 (refreshing, not heavy)

**Principle:** Anchor insights to real consumer language. Include original language when relevant.

> **PPTX note:** In slide format, select ONE hero verbatim per slide. Supporting quotes belong in speaker notes. The single quote should be large enough to read from the back of a conference room.

### 5. "If You Choose X" Framing

The packaging slide didn't say "Box is better than Cup." It said:

| Format | Context | Why |
|--------|---------|-----|
| Box | Office, formal sharing | Less childish, easier to store |
| Cup | Solo, leisure | Photogenic, risks being childish |

**Principle:** Frame decisions as trade-offs with consequences. Let leadership own the choice.

### 6. Structural Formulas

The document didn't just say "Mini-Thin works." It explained WHY:

```
Thin crispy biscuit + Sweet-sour filling = Removes sweetness ceiling
```

This is memorable, repeatable, and can be applied to future decisions.

**Principle:** The best insights are structural — they explain the mechanism, not just the outcome.

### 7. Honest Gaps

The document ended with "Work Still Needed":
- Quant validation
- Design round focused on munchability cues
- etc.

**Principle:** Acknowledging what you don't know builds credibility for what you do claim.

## Applying to Other Projects

When creating a decision document:

1. **Start with the Strategic Question** — What does leadership need to decide?
2. **Identify the Soul Question** — What will the skeptic say?
3. **Find the structural formula** — Not just "what" but "why"
4. **Use occasion-specific assessment** — Not "overall winner" but "where it wins"
5. **Frame decisions as trade-offs** — "If you choose X, then you need Y"
6. **Include consumer language** — Original terms, not just translations
7. **End with honest gaps** — What we still don't know
