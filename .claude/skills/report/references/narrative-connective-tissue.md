# Narrative Connective Tissue

How to make a slide deck feel like one argument instead of five separate presentations. Covers inter-section transitions, behavioral grounding for frameworks, and the common client feedback patterns that signal missing connective logic.

*Source: Chips Ahoy Premium Innovation report (Mar 2026). Lisa's feedback on v4 exposed six connective gaps — all resolved in v5.*

## The Problem

Client feedback that sounds like:
- "I don't get the logic from slide X to slide Y"
- "Why did we choose these [brands / dimensions / segments]?"
- "These are what we learned from competitors?" (said with confusion, not as a question)
- "Could you add some connections with the previous part?"

These are all the same problem: **missing connective tissue.** The content is right, but the audience can't see how one section leads to the next.

## Rule 1: Every Section Opener Connects Back

Every section's first content slide (the slide after the section divider) must include a **framing sentence** that answers two questions:
1. Why is this section here?
2. What did the previous section set up that makes this section necessary?

| Section Transition | Bad (Orphaned) | Good (Connected) |
|---|---|---|
| Consumer Landscape → Brand & Competitors | "Six brands in the competitive set" | "Six brands emerged as consumers described their reward & indulge moments — they define the landscape CA! must navigate" |
| Brand gaps → Concepts | "Five concepts tested" | "To address the needs of lapsed and competitor users, we tested five concepts — from ingredient swaps to texture transformations" |
| Concept results → Implications | "Implications" | "Two missions, two products — each earns premium for a different reason" |

**The test:** Read only the section divider + first content slide headline. Can someone who missed the previous section understand why this section exists? If not, add a framing sentence.

## Rule 2: Behavioral Grounding for Need States

When presenting need states, occasions, or consumer segments, include **behavioral markers** — not just emotional descriptions. Emotions explain *why*; behaviors explain *what actually happens* and give R&D/marketing something actionable.

### Required Behavioral Dimensions

For each need state or occasion, include at least 3 of these:

| Dimension | What It Captures | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Pace** | Speed of consumption | "抽空拿一块，然后继续搞" vs. "慢下来...为了享受才去吃的" |
| **Hands** | Physical engagement | Hands-free (叼到嘴巴里) vs. both hands engaged, breaking apart |
| **Setup** | Preparation ritual | Zero prep, package-to-mouth vs. "必须得有个盘子候着" |
| **Pairing** | What accompanies the snack | Nothing vs. tea, coffee, milk tea ("要仪式感") |
| **Attention** | Focus allocation | Background to task vs. "整个人瘫在沙发上...去享受我的零食" |
| **Duration** | Time spent | 3-5 minutes vs. 1-2 hours of grazing |
| **Social context** | Alone or with others | Solo desk eating vs. shared spread |

**Why this matters:** A client brief saying "target the reward occasion" is useless to a packaging team. "Reward = both hands, plate, paired with tea, 15+ minutes, solo" gives them a format brief.

### Format: Behavioral Contrast Table

The most effective format is a **contrast table** showing the behavioral difference between two need states side by side. This makes the framework tangible:

```
| Dimension | Low Energy / Functional | High Energy / Deliberate |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Pace      | Quick bite, 3-5 min    | Slow savour, 15+ min     |
| Hands     | One hand or hands-free | Both hands engaged        |
| Setup     | Package to mouth       | Plate, arrangement        |
| Pairing   | None                   | Tea, coffee, milk tea     |
```

Place the contrast table on the framework overview slide or on the first detail slide. It earns credibility for the entire framework.

## Rule 3: Competitive Set Selection Must Be Justified

When presenting a competitive set, the audience's first question is always: "Why these brands and not others?"

**Pattern:** The headline or subhead of the competitive set slide must connect brand selection to the analytical framework that preceded it.

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| "Key competitors" | "Six brands emerged as consumers described their reward & indulge moments" |
| "Competitive landscape" | "These brands define the competitive set because consumers named them in the occasions where CA! wants to win" |

If brands were chosen by the research team rather than emerging from consumer data, say so: "We selected these six based on [shelf adjacency / price band / occasion overlap] and validated with consumers."

## Rule 4: Section Transitions Signal Argument Moves

Section dividers in PPTX are natural rupture points — the audience's attention resets. Use the divider or the first post-divider slide to signal the **argument move**: what kind of thinking shift is about to happen.

Common argument moves in research presentations:

| From | To | Signal Phrase |
|---|---|---|
| Consumer landscape | Brand context | "Given these need states, where does the brand sit today?" |
| Brand gaps | Concept testing | "To close these gaps, we tested five innovation routes" |
| What didn't work | What worked | "The failures reveal the filter — here's what passes it" |
| Concept results | Implications | "Two concepts won for two different reasons — here's the portfolio logic" |
| Implications | Discussion | "Two open questions remain before quant" |

## Rule 5: Vocabulary Discipline

Once you name something, use that exact term every time you reference it. No synonyms, no paraphrasing, no drift.

**What this means:**
- If you call the need states Recharge, Refresh, Reward, and Indulge — those exact words appear on every slide that references them
- If you use Chinese labels (低谷唤醒, 平淡焕活, 愉悦增益, 主动犒劳), they accompany the English terms consistently
- If you assign colours or icons, they carry through every appearance
- If a concept is "C3 榛果奶巧 Hazelnut," it's never shortened to just "Hazelnut" or "C3" inconsistently

**Why this matters:** A framework becomes a shared language between you and the client. When the same term appears on the need-state map, the concept evaluation, and the portfolio architecture slide, the audience connects them instantly. When terms drift — calling it "mood uplift" in one section and "self-reward" in another — the audience loses the thread and starts wondering if these are the same thing or different things.

**The test:** Search your final deck for every instance of a framework term. Do they match exactly? If you introduced four named segments, do those four names appear identically wherever referenced?

## Rule 6: Same Insight, Three Lenses

A framework earns conviction through **triangulation** — showing the same insight from multiple angles, not repeating it. Each lens adds a dimension the others can't.

**Three lens types:**

| Lens | What It Shows | Format |
|---|---|---|
| **Conceptual** | The logic — how dimensions relate | 2x2 matrix, segmentation map, spectrum |
| **Behavioral** | The texture — what people actually do | Detail pages with verbatims, photos, behavioral markers |
| **Temporal** | The when — how it maps to real life | Day-part timeline, weekly rhythm, seasonal pattern |

**Pattern:**
```
First appearance (conceptual)  → audience understands the LOGIC
Second appearance (behavioral) → audience FEELS the framework through real behavior
Third appearance (temporal)    → audience sees WHERE IT LIVES in daily/weekly life
```

By the third viewing, the audience *owns* the framework. They're not hearing your analysis — they're seeing their consumers' lives through the lens you built.

**When to use:** Any time you introduce a multi-part framework (need states, segments, occasions) that the rest of the report depends on. The more the audience needs to internalise the framework to follow the argument, the more lenses you need.

**When NOT to use:** Simple findings that don't require a framework. Not every insight needs three passes — only the structural ones that carry the rest of the deck.

*Learned from: CA Premium report. The 4 need states appeared as a 2x2 framework (conceptual, slide 12), as detailed occasion pages with behaviours and verbatims (behavioral, slides 14-17), and as weekday/weekend day-part timelines (temporal, slides 20-21). By the time concepts were evaluated against need states, the audience had fully internalised the framework.*

## Checklist: Connective Tissue Audit

Before finalizing any deck, read through these checks:

- [ ] Every section's first content slide has a framing sentence connecting to the previous section
- [ ] Every analytical framework has a bridging sentence motivating the chosen dimensions
- [ ] Every need state includes at least 3 behavioral markers (not just emotional descriptions)
- [ ] The competitive set slide explains *why these brands*
- [ ] Section dividers or post-divider headlines signal the argument move
- [ ] A reader seeing only headlines + section transitions can follow the full argument
- [ ] Framework terms are identical every time they appear — no synonyms, no drift
- [ ] Key frameworks appear through at least 2 lenses (conceptual + behavioral, or conceptual + temporal)
