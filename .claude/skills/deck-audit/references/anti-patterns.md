# Deck Anti-Patterns

Common patterns that undermine presentation effectiveness. Flag these during audits.

## Narrative Anti-Patterns

### The Thesis Wander

**What it looks like**: Deck starts with one argument, ends with a different one.

**Why it fails**: Reader feels lost; recommendations don't connect to opening.

**Diagnosis**: Compare slide 3's implied promise to the final recommendation. Do they match?

**Fix**: Rewrite opening to match actual conclusion, or restructure argument.

---

### The Evidence Pile

**What it looks like**: 15 data slides, then suddenly "Recommendations."

**Why it fails**: No synthesis; reader must do interpretive work.

**Diagnosis**: Count slides between last data slide and first recommendation. Should be 1-3 synthesis slides.

**Fix**: Add "What this means" bridge slides.

---

### Pyramid Inversion

**What it looks like**: Recommendations first, then evidence.

**Why it fails**: Audience hasn't bought in; feels like assertion without proof.

**Diagnosis**: Do recommendations appear before supporting evidence?

**Fix**: Lead with problem and evidence; earn the right to recommend.

**Exception**: When audience specifically requests "bottom line up front" and has high trust.

---

### The Appendix Graveyard

**What it looks like**: Key evidence buried in 30-slide appendix.

**Why it fails**: Won't be read; weakens core argument.

**Diagnosis**: Is there critical data in the appendix that the core argument depends on?

**Fix**: Pull critical evidence forward. Appendix is for reference, not argument.

---

### The Missing "So What"

**What it looks like**: Lots of data, no interpretation.

**Why it fails**: Reader must derive their own conclusions; often won't.

**Diagnosis**: After each data slide, is there a "this means..." statement?

**Fix**: Add insight callouts; make interpretations explicit.

---

## Slide-Level Anti-Patterns

### Topic Label Titles

**What it looks like**: "Market Overview," "Consumer Insights," "Recommendations"

**Why it fails**: Reader doesn't know the point without reading the body.

**Diagnosis**: Can you tell the story from titles alone?

**Fix**: Rewrite as claims: "Premium segment grew 3x faster than mass market"

---

### The Kitchen Sink

**What it looks like**: 6 bullets on unrelated topics.

**Why it fails**: No clear takeaway; reader retains nothing.

**Diagnosis**: Ask "what's the one point?" If you need "and," it's overloaded.

**Fix**: Split into 2-3 focused slides.

---

### Wall of Text

**What it looks like**: 8+ bullets, each 2+ lines. Dense paragraphs.

**Why it fails**: Won't be read; audience loses attention.

**Diagnosis**: Word count per slide. Over 75 words is a warning; over 100 is a problem.

**Fix**: Cut ruthlessly. If you can't cut, split slides.

---

### Orphan Data

**What it looks like**: Chart with no callout, no interpretation.

**Why it fails**: Reader must interpret; often interprets wrong or not at all.

**Diagnosis**: Any chart without annotation or insight?

**Fix**: Add callout stating what the chart proves.

---

### The False Choice

**What it looks like**: "We can either do A or B" when more options exist.

**Why it fails**: Manipulative framing; damages trust.

**Diagnosis**: Are choices artificially constrained?

**Fix**: Present honest range of options, then recommend.

---

### Feature Creep Slide

**What it looks like**: "Our product has 47 features" with long bulleted list.

**Why it fails**: Undifferentiated; no reason to care.

**Diagnosis**: Is the list just features with no benefit framing?

**Fix**: Focus on 3-5 that matter to this audience; frame as benefits.

---

## Visual Anti-Patterns

### Chartjunk

**What it looks like**: 3D effects, clip art, decorative gradients.

**Why it fails**: Distracts from data; looks amateur.

**Diagnosis**: Could any visual element be removed without losing information?

**Fix**: Strip to essentials.

---

### The Rainbow Chart

**What it looks like**: Every bar/slice a different color with no logic.

**Why it fails**: Color should encode meaning, not be decorative.

**Diagnosis**: What does each color mean? If no answer, it's noise.

**Fix**: Use intentional color palette; limit to 3-4 colors.

---

### Truncated Axes

**What it looks like**: Bar chart where y-axis starts at 90%, not 0%.

**Why it fails**: Exaggerates differences; can mislead.

**Diagnosis**: Does the y-axis start at zero? If not, is it labeled?

**Fix**: Start at zero, or clearly mark the break.

---

### Death by Bullet

**What it looks like**: Every slide is a bulleted list.

**Why it fails**: Monotonous; visual fatigue.

**Diagnosis**: Count consecutive bullet-list slides. More than 3-4 is a warning.

**Fix**: Vary formats: frameworks, diagrams, pull quotes, data.

---

### Template Captivity

**What it looks like**: Every slide identical layout, regardless of content.

**Why it fails**: Content should drive layout, not the reverse.

**Diagnosis**: Are there layouts that feel forced into the template?

**Fix**: Override template when content needs different treatment.

---

## Evidence Anti-Patterns

### The Lonely Stat

**What it looks like**: Major claim supported by one data point.

**Why it fails**: Cherry-picking; vulnerable to challenge.

**Diagnosis**: How many sources support each major claim?

**Fix**: Triangulate with multiple sources; address limitations.

---

### The Stale Source

**What it looks like**: "Source: McKinsey, 2019" for a 2025 market claim.

**Why it fails**: May no longer be accurate; looks lazy.

**Diagnosis**: Are key claims supported by sources more than 2-3 years old?

**Fix**: Find current sources; if none exist, caveat the data.

---

### The Missing Source

**What it looks like**: "Market is worth $50B" with no citation.

**Why it fails**: Unverifiable; damages credibility.

**Diagnosis**: Every key number should have a source.

**Fix**: Add sources; if you can't find one, caveat or remove.

---

### The Convenient Sample

**What it looks like**: "9 out of 10 customers prefer..." (n=10)

**Why it fails**: Sample too small; not generalizable.

**Diagnosis**: Is sample size adequate for the claim being made?

**Fix**: Note sample limitations; be appropriately hedged.

---

### Self-Serving Selection

**What it looks like**: Only data that supports the argument; counter-evidence ignored.

**Why it fails**: Audience may know more; damages trust when discovered.

**Diagnosis**: Is there evidence against this argument? Is it acknowledged?

**Fix**: Address counter-evidence proactively; show why it doesn't undermine conclusion.

---

## Structure Anti-Patterns

### The 50-Slide Deck

**What it looks like**: Everything you know on the topic.

**Why it fails**: Attention lost after slide 15; key message buried.

**Diagnosis**: Could this be 15 slides plus appendix?

**Fix**: Ruthlessly cut. Core deck should be 10-20 slides.

---

### Mirror Structure

**What it looks like**: 5 competitors, each gets identical 3-slide profile.

**Why it fails**: Mechanical; suggests no real insight.

**Diagnosis**: Is structure repetitive in a way that adds no value?

**Fix**: Synthesize across cases; save profiles for appendix.

---

### The Missing Executive Summary

**What it looks like**: Deck launches straight into content.

**Why it fails**: Reader can't grasp the argument before diving in.

**Diagnosis**: Can a busy exec get the point from slides 1-2?

**Fix**: Add exec summary or "key findings" upfront.

---

### The Filler Section

**What it looks like**: 4 slides on methodology no one asked about.

**Why it fails**: Wastes attention; often defensive inclusion.

**Diagnosis**: Could this section be cut or moved to appendix without harm?

**Fix**: Cut or condense to one slide if needed.

---

### The Agenda Obsession

**What it looks like**: Detailed agenda, section dividers, mid-point agendas.

**Why it fails**: Wastes slides; breaks narrative flow.

**Diagnosis**: How many slides are meta-content vs. actual content?

**Fix**: Single agenda if deck is long; otherwise, let content flow.

---

## Recommendation Anti-Patterns

### The Recommendation Pile

**What it looks like**: 12 recommendations, unprioritized.

**Why it fails**: Reader doesn't know where to start; nothing stands out.

**Diagnosis**: Are recommendations clearly prioritized?

**Fix**: Tier into P1/P2/P3; focus on 3-5 core recs.

---

### The Vague Recommendation

**What it looks like**: "Improve customer experience."

**Why it fails**: Not actionable; could mean anything.

**Diagnosis**: Could you tell if this was implemented?

**Fix**: Make specific: "Reduce checkout steps from 5 to 3 by Q3."

---

### The Unearned Recommendation

**What it looks like**: Recommendation that wasn't set up by evidence.

**Why it fails**: Feels random; reader doesn't buy in.

**Diagnosis**: Can you trace each recommendation to specific evidence in the deck?

**Fix**: Add the evidentiary bridge; or cut the rec.

---

### The Obvious Recommendation

**What it looks like**: "Continue to focus on quality."

**Why it fails**: Adds no value; anyone could say it.

**Diagnosis**: Would a competitor be surprised by this advice?

**Fix**: Push for specificity; if the insight is weak, the rec will be too.

---

## Presentation Context Anti-Patterns

### Reading the Slides

**What it looks like**: Slides that must be read aloud to make sense.

**Why it fails**: Presenter adds no value; could send email instead.

**Diagnosis**: Can slides stand alone AND support live presentation?

**Fix**: Slides are visual aids; key points stated verbally.

---

### The Dual-Purpose Deck

**What it looks like**: Deck tries to be both leave-behind and presentation.

**Why it fails**: Neither goal achieved well.

**Diagnosis**: Does deck have too much text for presenting, too little for reading?

**Fix**: Create two versions, or use appendix for detail.

---

### No Clear Ask

**What it looks like**: Deck ends without stating what's needed from audience.

**Why it fails**: No clear next step; momentum dies.

**Diagnosis**: Does the final slide have a clear call to action?

**Fix**: End with specific ask: decision needed, resources required, next meeting.
