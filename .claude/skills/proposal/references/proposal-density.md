# Proposal Density Reduction (PPTX)

How to reduce proposal prose to PowerPoint billboard density. Proposals are naturally Word-density documents — detailed, persuasive, information-rich. The PPTX path must strip them to billboard weight without losing the argument.

## The Problem

Proposals written in draft mode are full prose: multi-paragraph thought-starters, detailed methodology explanations, narrative sample rationales. This is perfect for Word. Projected on screen, it becomes a wall of text that no one reads.

## The Density Pass

Before rendering any proposal section as PPTX, run each slide through these transformations:

### 1. Headlines Become Claims

| Before (topic label) | After (claim) |
|---|---|
| Methodology | A three-phase approach grounds cultural insight before consumer validation |
| Sample Design | Eight carefully profiled consumers across two cities reveal both aspiration and reality |
| Executive Summary | Inner Chapter will decode the cultural DNA of premium in three markets |
| Thought-starter 1 | Premium isn't a price point — it's a permission structure |

**Test:** Cover the slide body. Does the headline alone tell the story? If it just names a topic, rewrite it as a finding or assertion.

### 2. Paragraphs Become Bullets

Every paragraph in the proposal must become either:
- **3-5 bullets** (if the content stays on the slide face)
- **`[NOTES:]` block** (if the content is supporting evidence)

No prose paragraphs on the slide face. Ever.

### 3. Evidence Moves to Notes

Proposal sections are evidence-heavy by nature. For PPTX:
- **Slide face:** The verdict, the claim, the "so what"
- **`[NOTES:]` block:** The supporting data, the methodology detail, the full rationale

```markdown
## A three-phase approach grounds cultural insight before consumer validation

- Phase 1: Cultural & semiotic foundation (4 weeks)
- Phase 2: Consumer immersion across 3 cities (3 weeks)
- Phase 3: Synthesis, frameworks, and client delivery (3 weeks)

[HINT: visual timeline — 3 phases flowing left to right, each with icon + duration badge — muted functional — no tables]

[NOTES:]
Phase 1 detail:
- Desk research covering competitive landscape, cultural codes, and category semiotics
- 4 expert interviews: 2 industry, 1 cultural, 1 academic
- Stimulus development for Phase 2 consumer sessions
- Output: Hypotheses document, discussion guide, stimulus deck

Phase 2 detail:
- 12 IDIs across Shanghai (6) and Beijing (6)
- Pre-task: 3-day digital diary capturing premium moments
- Post-session stimulus sort for concept validation
- Output: Topline findings, consumer insight cards

Phase 3 detail:
- Cross-source triangulation: experts × consumers × semiotics
- Framework development and internal stress-test
- Two client deliverables: oral debrief + full report
[/NOTES]
```

### 4. Tables Become Visual Metaphors

Proposal tables (methodology comparison, sample breakdown, investment) are often too dense for slides.

| Table Type | PPTX Alternative |
|---|---|
| Methodology phases | Visual timeline with phase blocks |
| Sample profiles | Character cards (one per profile) |
| Sample quotas | Iconography trio (hero numbers: total n, cities, segments) |
| Investment breakdown | Keep as table — one of the few exceptions |
| Deliverables list | Ascending step-ladder or numbered cards |
| Timeline/Gantt | Visual timeline with milestone markers |

**Exception:** Investment tables stay as tables in PPTX. This is one of the few cases where a table IS the right encoding — clients expect line-item pricing in tabular format.

### 5. Thought-starters Get the Flash Treatment

Thought-starters are the most prose-heavy proposal section. For PPTX, each thought-starter becomes 2 slides:

**Slide 1: The Setup**
- Headline = provocative claim (not "Thought-starter 1")
- Body = 2-3 evidence bullets
- One data point or cultural reference

**Slide 2: The Strategic Question**
- "How might we...?" at billboard scale
- No other content — let the question land

The full thought-starter prose (cultural context, examples, data) goes into `[NOTES:]` on slide 1.

### 6. Case Studies Become Impact Cards

Full case study prose → split 30/70 layout:
- Left: Client name + project type
- Right: Challenge (1 line) / Approach (3 bullets) / Impact (1 line)

The detailed narrative moves to `[NOTES:]`.

## Density Limits (from ic-slides)

| Element | Maximum | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Bullets | 3-5 per slide | If you need 6, split the slide |
| Words per bullet | 12-15 | One clause, not a sentence |
| Hero quote | 1 per slide | Large enough to read from back of room |
| Tables | 3 cols × 4 rows | Beyond this → simplify or move to notes |
| Prose paragraphs on slide face | 0 | Prose belongs in speaker notes |
| Distinct visual elements | ≤ 4 | Quote + bullets + table = overloaded |

## The Proposal-Specific Trap

Proposals are selling documents. The instinct is to include everything to be persuasive. On slides, this backfires — density signals insecurity, not thoroughness.

**Word proposal density = thoroughness.** The client reads at their own pace, revisits sections, forwards to colleagues.

**PPTX proposal density = confidence.** The presenter narrates the rationale. The slide shows the headline verdict. Less text = more authority.

The density pass is not about removing content. It's about relocating it — from the slide face (where it competes for attention) to `[NOTES:]` (where the presenter draws on it live).
